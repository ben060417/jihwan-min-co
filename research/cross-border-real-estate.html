Why cross border real estate is harder than it looks

Currency, regulation, and trust, the three frictions that quietly decide returns.

9 min read  ·  Capital Markets Research

The pitch that hides the problem

Cross-border real estate is sold as a simple arbitrage. Capital is cheap in one place and assets yield more in another, so you move the money and pocket the difference. That story is true for exactly as long as nothing moves, and something always moves. The reason cross-border deals disappoint is rarely the building. It is the three frictions that sit underneath the arbitrage and quietly decide the return: currency, regulation, and trust.

Each of these is invisible in the pitch deck and decisive in the outcome. A firm that understands them does not necessarily do more deals. It does fewer bad ones, which over a full cycle is the same thing as doing better.

Currency: the return you did not underwrite

The first friction is the one everyone acknowledges and almost everyone underestimates. When a Korean institution buys an American asset, its return is denominated in dollars but measured in won. The gap between those two is a second, uninvited investment sitting on top of the real estate, and it is often larger and more volatile than the property return itself. Hedging closes the gap, but hedging has a cost, and that cost moves. In a calm year the hedge is a rounding error. In a stressed year it can consume the entire spread that justified the deal in the first place. The asset can perform exactly as underwritten and the investor can still lose, purely on the currency leg.

Regulation: two rulebooks that do not agree

The second friction is that a cross-border deal lives under two legal and tax systems that were not designed to cooperate. Ownership structures that are efficient on one side of the Pacific can be punishing on the other. Withholding taxes, entity classification, repatriation rules, and reporting obligations all have to be reconciled before a single dollar of profit comes home. This is slow, expensive, and easy to get wrong, and the cost of getting it wrong is usually discovered years later, at exit, when it is too late to restructure. The regulatory friction does not announce itself. It waits.

Trust: the friction nobody prices

The third friction is the hardest to model and the most important. A cross-border deal is a relationship between parties who cannot easily verify each other. The Korean allocator cannot walk the American submarket every quarter. The American sponsor cannot read the allocator’s internal committee dynamics. Each has to trust that the other is representing the deal honestly, and that trust has to survive translation across language, distance, and two different professional cultures. When it holds, everything else becomes negotiable. When it fails, no structure and no hedge can save the deal.

This is why so much of the durable cross-border activity flows through a narrow set of intermediaries who have earned trust on both sides. They are not adding value by finding buildings. They are adding value by being the party both sides can believe, which is the scarcest input in the entire trade.

Why the frictions are the opportunity

It is tempting to read all of this as a warning to stay home. It is closer to the opposite. The frictions are precisely why the corridor is not efficient, and inefficiency is where advantage lives. The firm that can genuinely hold the currency view, navigate both rulebooks, and carry trust across the gap is not competing on price. It is competing on a capability that most participants simply do not have. The arbitrage that looked simple in the pitch was never the real opportunity. The real opportunity is the friction, for whoever is equipped to absorb it.
