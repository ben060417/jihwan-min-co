What a capital stack actually tells you about a deal

Equity, mezzanine, and senior debt, and what their order says about risk.

7 min read  ·  Capital Markets Research

The stack is an order, not a list

When a deal is described as having equity, mezzanine debt, and senior debt, the temptation is to read those as three ingredients. They are not ingredients. They are an order of who gets paid and who absorbs loss first, and that order tells you almost everything about how the people who structured the deal actually think about its risk. Read from the bottom up, the capital stack is a confession. It says what the sponsor was afraid of and what the lender demanded in exchange for showing up.

Every dollar in a real estate deal sits somewhere in that order. Cash flows fill obligations from the top of the stack down, and losses eat the stack from the bottom up. Once you internalize that single mechanic, the labels stop being jargon and start being a map of risk.

Senior debt: the floor everyone else stands on

Senior debt sits at the bottom of the loss order and the top of the payment order. It gets paid first and loses last, which is why it is the cheapest capital in the deal. The senior lender is not trying to make a real estate return. It is trying to make a fixed return with a large cushion of other people’s money underneath it. The size of that cushion, the gap between the loan and the value of the asset, is the whole game for the senior lender. When you see a conservative loan-to-value, you are seeing a lender who wanted a lot of room before its dollar was ever at risk.

Mezzanine: the price of the gap

Mezzanine debt exists because the senior lender refused to lend the whole amount and the equity did not want to fund the difference itself. It fills the gap between what senior debt will cover and what the equity is willing to put in. It is more expensive than senior debt because it loses sooner, and it is cheaper than equity because it loses later. Its very presence is a signal. A thick mezzanine layer usually means the sponsor was stretching, that the equity wanted to control a larger asset than its own capital could support, and paid for the privilege with a costly middle layer.

Equity: first to lose, last to be paid

Equity sits at the bottom of the payment order and the top of the loss order. It absorbs the first dollar of loss and collects only after everyone else is satisfied. In exchange, it keeps the upside. Everything above a fixed return flows to equity once the debt is paid. This is why equity holders behave the way they do: they are the ones with the most to gain and the most to lose, and their willingness to sit in that seat is the market’s clearest vote on whether a deal is any good.

Reading a deal from its stack

Once you know the order, a capital stack becomes a diagnostic. A deal financed almost entirely with conservative senior debt and a thick slug of equity is a deal whose sponsor expected to hold through turbulence. A deal loaded with mezzanine and thin equity is a deal built for a specific, and usually short, window, one that needed everything to go right. Neither is wrong. But the stack tells you which bet was being made before you read a single line of the business plan.

The next time a deal is put in front of you, resist reading the projections first. Read the stack. It will tell you what the people closest to the deal were actually worried about, and that is a more honest document than any pro forma.
