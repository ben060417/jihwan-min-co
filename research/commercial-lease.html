Reading a commercial lease like an underwriter

The five clauses that decide whether a building is worth what the broker says it is.

8 min read  ·  Capital Markets Research

A broker sells a building on its headline number: the cap rate, the price per square foot, the rent roll on the day you tour it. An underwriter reads the same building and asks a different question. Not what does it earn today, but what is it contractually obligated to earn tomorrow, and who bears the cost when reality diverges from the pro forma. That answer lives in the lease, not the marketing package.

Five clauses carry most of the weight.

1. Rent escalations

The single most consequential line in any lease is how rent grows. A flat annual bump and an increase tied to CPI produce very different buildings over a ten year hold, especially in an inflationary stretch. Fixed escalations are predictable but can lag the market; index-linked escalations protect the landlord but unsettle tenants. What matters for value is not the starting rent but the compounding path, and whether that path was negotiated in a market that looks anything like today's.

2. Recoveries and the expense stop

Who pays for taxes, insurance, and maintenance decides whether the stated net operating income is real. A true triple-net lease pushes those costs to the tenant; a gross lease with an expense stop leaves the landlord exposed to everything above a fixed base. Two buildings with identical headline rents can carry very different owner economics depending on where that line sits.

3. Tenant improvement and concession obligations

Signing a tenant is not the same as being paid by one. Free rent periods, tenant improvement allowances, and moving costs are real capital the landlord commits up front and recovers slowly. A lease that looks strong on face rent may be far weaker on effective rent once concessions are amortized across the term.

4. Options: renewal, termination, and expansion

Options are optionality held by the tenant, and optionality carries a cost borne by the landlord. An early termination right converts a ten year lease into a three year lease for valuation purposes. A below-market renewal option caps upside precisely when the market would otherwise reward the owner. These are the clauses brokers mention last and underwriters read first.

5. Assignment, subletting, and use

Finally, who can occupy the space and under what conditions determines how liquid the income stream is. Tight use restrictions and broad landlord consent rights protect the asset's character but narrow the pool of replacement tenants. The clause reads as boilerplate; it prices as risk.

None of this is exotic. It is simply the discipline of reading the document that actually governs the cash flow, rather than the summary written by the party trying to sell it. A building is worth what its lease says it will earn, adjusted for the probability that each clause plays out as written. Everything else is a headline.
